How to Read the Results

Everything on this page assumes you started with your chosen initial capital (e.g. ₹1,00,000). Here is what each number means in plain language.

Total Return & CAGR

Total Return is the simplest number — if it says -36%, you turned ₹1,00,000 into ₹64,000.

CAGR (Compound Annual Growth Rate) tells you the average yearly growth rate. -18% CAGR means your money shrank by 18% every year on average. A bank FD gives ~7% CAGR, so anything below that isn't beating a savings account.

Max Drawdown

Your portfolio grows and shrinks over time. Drawdown measures how far you have fallen from your previous best point — not from the start.

Example: your ₹1,00,000 grows to ₹1,10,000, then falls to ₹60,000. That is a -45% drawdown from the ₹1,10,000 peak — even though you only started at ₹1,00,000.

Max Drawdown is the worst this ever got. It tells you the most stomach-dropping moment you would have lived through holding this strategy.

Sharpe & Calmar Ratios

Both ratios answer: "was the return worth the risk?"

Sharpe compares return vs daily ups-and-downs (volatility). Above 1.0 = good. Below 0 = losing money for the risk taken.

Calmar compares CAGR vs Max Drawdown. Above 1.0 = the yearly gain is bigger than the worst loss you'd face. Below 0 = the strategy is losing while also drawing down badly.

Win Rate & Profit Factor

Win Rate is simply how often you won. 25% win rate means 1 in 4 trades made money. A low win rate is not necessarily fatal — some strategies win rarely but win big.

Profit Factor is the real test: for every ₹1 you lost, how much did you make back? 0.60 means you earned ₹0.60 back for every ₹1 lost — a losing strategy. You want this above 1.5. Above 2.0 is excellent.

Reading the Charts

Equity Curve (green vs dashed)

The green line is your strategy's portfolio value over time, starting at your initial capital.

The dashed white line is what would have happened if you just bought and held the stock from day one — the benchmark.

When the green line is above the dashed line, the strategy beat the market. When it's below, Buy & Hold would have served you better.

Drawdown Chart (red)

The red chart starts at 0% (you are at a new all-time high) and dips down whenever the portfolio falls from its peak.

Example: the line drops to -30% — at that moment, your portfolio is 30% below whatever its best-ever value was at that point in time.

When the line recovers back toward 0%, the strategy has clawed back to a new peak. A strategy that spends a long time deep in negative territory is painful and risky to hold in real life.

Trade Log

Each row is one complete trade — the strategy bought on the Entry date and sold on the Exit date.

P&L % is the percentage gain or loss on that specific trade. The Days column shows how long you were in that position.

Look for patterns: are losses held too long? Are winners cut too early? A 1-day trade that lost money means the signal triggered and reversed almost immediately — a sign of noise in the strategy.

Buy & Hold Benchmark Row

This row shows the same metrics for a simple Buy & Hold of the stock — no strategy, just hold forever.

It is the bar you need to clear. If your strategy's CAGR, Sharpe, and Drawdown are all worse than Buy & Hold, the strategy added no value — you'd have been better off doing nothing.

A strategy that loses money but loses less than Buy & Hold (with a smaller drawdown) is still providing downside protection — which has real value in volatile markets.

How the Simulation Works

🤔

1. The "What If"

The engine replays history day by day. On each day it only looks at data available up to that point — no peeking at the future. It runs The Sicilian's signals and decides: Buy, Sell, or Hold.

🖱️

2. Realistic Costs

Every buy and sell deducts 0.1% commission + 0.05% slippage (the difference between the price you see and the price you actually get filled at). This prevents the simulation from being unrealistically perfect.

⏩

3. Long-Only

The engine is long-only — it either owns the stock (position = 1) or sits in cash (position = 0). A SELL signal means "exit to cash", not "short sell". This matches how a retail investor in India actually operates.

⚠️

4. Limitations

Past performance does not guarantee future results. Backtests can look good by accident (overfitting). Always check: does the strategy beat Buy & Hold? Is the drawdown acceptable? Are there enough trades to be statistically meaningful?
